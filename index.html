Dean Cascione is a master luthier and professional guitarist who has been hand-building instruments and performing world-class fretboard scalloping since 2004. Every instrument that leaves the DC Custom Guitars shop is built, finished, wired, assembled, and set up by Dean himself.

From a single sketch on a napkin to the final setup, you work hand-in-hand with Dean through the entire build process. You choose the body wood, neck profile, fretboard, inlays, pickups, bridge, tuners, hardware, and finish. The result: a guitar that looks, feels, and sounds like exactly what you imagined.

What sets Dean apart from traditional builders? Most luthiers come from a woodworking background — building and setting up guitars to specification. Some builders don't even play guitar. Dean takes the build one step further, leveraging his technical command of the instrument to play, tweak, and tune every guitar — validating its shredding and sonic capabilities prior to delivery. Every guitar that leaves the DC Custom Shop is ready to shred.

DC Custom has also been trusted by some of the most respected names in shred — and Dean's scalloping clients include pro players and OEM companies worldwide.

Fretboard Scalloping

Unleash the secrets

All work performed by hand, in our shop, by Dean himself. Trusted by pro players and OEM companies worldwide since 2004.

Hand-scalloped, the way it should be.

We do hand scalloping — following the natural radius of the neck. A radius hand-scalloped neck is more comfortable to play and leaves more wood intact, which means a stronger, straighter neck that stays in tune.

Experienced in set necks, bolt-on necks, and neck-through guitars — 6, 7, 8+ string, fanned fret, and multi-scale necks, and just about any dot inlay configuration (dots, shark fins, abalone, blocks). Scalloping services are also offered to many OEM companies.

Every scallop is finished with the right treatment for the wood: nitrocellulose lacquer and sealer for maple, tongue oil for rosewood and ebony families, or left bare if you prefer.

Scalloping FAQ

The details.
Straight up.

What is neck scalloping?

Neck scalloping is the removal of fretboard wood between each fret. The two popular methods are barrel / straight-through automated machining and hand scalloping, which follows the neck radius. DC Custom offers hand scalloping because a radius hand-scalloped neck is more natural and comfortable to play, and leaves more wood intact — meaning a stronger, straighter neck. When complete, the fretboard looks identical to the scalloped shredder custom-shop necks found on high-end guitars.

Why scallop?

The most noticeable advantage is more control of the notes you fret — better tone and a cleaner sound, no matter how fast you're playing. It also means more control when bending and using vibrato / pull-on-off techniques. Your fingers wrap around the string instead of bottoming out on a flat fretboard, giving you more surface area gripping the string and complete control of each note.

Full vs. progressive scallop (Blackmore vs. YJM)

The Blackmore style is a progressive scallop — from the side it looks similar to a "Nike Swoosh." It has little to no scallop under the bass strings (EAD) and gets progressively deeper under the treble strings (GBE), contouring into a full scallop after the 12th fret. It leaves more wood intact, helping players who fret chords with heavy hands stay in tune.

The YJM style is a full pipe scallop — like a BMX or skateboard ramp that extends from fret to fret. All the wood is removed, giving your fingers maximum bite around every string across the entire register.

Scallop depth & shape

Side-dot installation depth plays an important role in the actual scallop depth. Necks can typically be scalloped to a depth of 1–2mm without affecting side dots. Blackmore progressive, YJM full, 12th-fret-up, 9th-fret-up, and 21–24th-fret scallops are our most popular types — and we're happy to work with you to create your own custom scallop shape.

Which fretboard woods do you accept?

All fretboard wood types are accepted, including Indian & Brazilian Rosewood; Quartersawn, Hardrock, Flame, and Birdseye Maple; Cocobolo; Kingwood; Zebrawood; Pau Ferro; Ebony; and more.

Will you re-finish the fretboard after scalloping?

Yes. We use nitrocellulose lacquer & sealer on maple fretboards, and tongue oil on fretboards that are normally oiled (rosewood, ebony, etc.). We can also leave the fretboard bare if you prefer.

Turnaround time

Typically 2–3 weeks for rosewood-family woods and 3–4 weeks for maple and ebony, from the time the neck is received at the shop. Set-neck and neck-through guitars require additional time to complete.

Shipping — why am I charged, and how is it sent?

We ship FedEx, USPS, and UPS — least expensive for you to keep costs down (ground shipping, no insurance by default). A flat fee of $30 per neck and $75 per guitar is added to ship your package back. If you'd like faster service, partial or exact shipping insurance, or to use your own shipping account, send us a label and we'll work with you on the safest, fastest method.

Are there risks?

Yes — there are always risks when modifying a stock guitar neck and shipping to and from our business. Manufacturers can vary their tolerances over time (fretboard thickness, inlay depth, inlay glue / installation materials), which may impact the scalloping process.

Shipping services can also be delayed due to circumstances beyond our control, and packages may get lost. DC Custom Guitars is not responsible for purchasing, refunding, building, or replacing a lost or damaged instrument. Extra shipping insurance must be purchased by the client and shipping upgraded to cover the risk before the instrument or parts leave our shop. If an issue arises, we'll work with all carriers to help find your package and make sure it arrives safely.
